Overview of COMPOSE. Given an anchor paper, COMPOSE builds a scientific citation subgraph Gs and a Mathlib formal theorem subgraph Gf. Abstract

Anticipating future mathematical claims requires more than extrapolating from prior text: progress is shaped both by evolving scientific ideas and by formal dependency structures that constrain valid results. Existing approaches typically capture only one of these signals.

We introduce grounded future mathematical generation, the task of predicting plausible future claims from an anchor paper by jointly leveraging citation context and formal theorem structure. We construct a dataset of 108K paired scientific–formal graph examples from arXiv and Mathlib (up to 2023), along with a temporally grounded benchmark of 47K future papers from 2024–2025.

We propose COMPOSE, a dual-graph framework that builds and fuses scientific and formal graphs to condition a language model for theorem-like prediction. COMPOSE outperforms strong baselines, producing more grounded and mathematically faithful predictions, and achieves the best retrieval performance — recovering the correct future paper in the top 10 in 75.0% of cases on the confidence-stratified evaluation subset.

Method

COMPOSE processes each anchor paper through two parallel paths:

  • Scientific graph Gs: up to 30 cited papers, each represented by its E5-large-v2 abstract embedding. A SimpleGNN produces 1152-dim node representations.
  • Formal graph Gf: a subgraph of Mathlib4 theorems related to the paper, identified by cosine similarity to the abstract and expanded via BFS through proof dependencies. Nodes are initialized from DeepSeek-Math embeddings of theorem statements.

Both graphs are projected into a shared 4224-dim space and fused via bidirectional cross-attention. The fused node representations are injected into DeepSeek-Math-7B as cross-attention keys/values at 8 evenly-spaced decoder layers. Only the cross-attention weights are trained (10.7% of parameters), using a two-stage curriculum: graph pretraining with contrastive + alignment losses, then generation fine-tuning with InfoNCE + margin loss.
