from Citations and Formal Structure

David Busbib, Michael Werman
Hebrew University of Jerusalem

Abstract

Anticipating future mathematical claims requires more than extrapolating from prior text: progress is shaped both by evolving scientific ideas and by formal dependency structures that constrain valid results. Existing approaches typically capture only one of these signals.

We introduce grounded future mathematical generation, the task of predicting plausible future claims from an anchor paper by jointly leveraging citation context and formal theorem structure. We construct a dataset of 108K paired scientific–formal graph examples from arXiv and Mathlib (up to 2023), along with a temporally grounded benchmark of 47K future papers from 2024–2025.

We propose COMPOSE, a dual-graph framework that builds and fuses scientific and formal graphs to condition a language model for theorem-like prediction. COMPOSE outperforms strong baselines, producing more grounded and mathematically faithful predictions, and achieves the best retrieval performance — recovering the correct future paper in the top 10 in 75.0% of cases.

Grounded Future Mathematical Generation

Standard future-paper prediction treats the problem as language modelling over abstracts. COMPOSE re-frames it as a graph-conditioned generation task: the model is given the full citation neighbourhood of an anchor paper together with the formal Mathlib theorems closest to its topic, and must generate a theorem-level claim that will be proved in a future paper.

Formally, each example is a triple (Gs, Gf, y*), where Gs is the scientific citation subgraph, Gf is the Mathlib theorem dependency subgraph, and y* is the abstract or main claim of the future paper used as the retrieval target.

Citation Graph Gs

Up to 30 cited papers, each embedded with E5-large-v2 (1024-dim). Edges encode citation direction. A SimpleGNN produces 1152-dim contextualized node representations.

Formal Graph Gf

Top-K Mathlib theorems by cosine similarity to the abstract, expanded via BFS through proof dependencies. Nodes are initialized from DeepSeek-Math theorem embeddings.

Evaluation

Generated claims are embedded with a fine-tuned DeepSeek-Math model and used to retrieve the actual future paper from a 47K pool. Primary metric: H@10.

Dataset

We build 108K paired scientific–formal graph examples from arXiv (2000–2023) and Mathlib4. For each paper, we align its informal theorem statements to Mathlib theorems using a two-stage pipeline: semantic embedding search followed by formal-syntax filtering. Papers with at least one high-confidence Mathlib match are included in training; the rest fall back to citation-only mode.

The benchmark uses a strict temporal split: all evaluation papers are from 2024–2025 (never seen during training), and we retain only anchor papers cited by at least 7 future papers in the pool, yielding approximately 2K test queries against a 47K-paper retrieval pool.

COMPOSE Architecture

Both graph encoders share the same SimpleGNN message-passing architecture but differ in node initialization. Enc1 uses E5-large-v2 abstract embeddings; enc2 uses DeepSeek-Math embeddings of Lean theorem statements. After two GNN hops, node representations are projected via a Bridge MLP into a shared 4224-dim latent space.

A single bidirectional cross-attention block then lets the scientific graph attend to the formal graph and vice versa, producing fused representations that capture both dimensions simultaneously. These fused representations are injected as cross-attention keys/values into 8 evenly-spaced layers of DeepSeek-Math-7B, with only the cross-attention weights trained (10.7% of parameters).

Training follows a two-stage curriculum: (1) graph pretraining with contrastive link-prediction, cross-graph alignment, and InfoNCE losses; (2) generation fine-tuning with a cross-entropy generation loss and a margin loss that prevents the decoder from ignoring the graph context.

Stage 1 — Graph Pretraining

Train enc1 and enc2 jointly using link prediction (Llink), cross-domain alignment (Lalign), and informal↔formal contrastive loss (Lcross). Decoder is frozen.

Stage 2 — Generation Fine-tuning

Unfreeze cross-attention weights in DeepSeek-Math-7B. Train with LCE (autoregressive) + Lmargin (penalise ignoring the graph by comparing losses on matched vs. mismatched graph pairs).

Decoder cross-attention. Fused graph node representations are projected to the decoder hidden dimension and injected at layers 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, 23, 27, 31. Each generation step attends over the full set of fused graph nodes.

Limitations and Future Work

Our alignment pipeline achieves high-confidence Mathlib matches for only 16.9% of paper theorems; for the rest, the model falls back to the scientific graph alone (H@10 drops from 0.750 to 0.075 without Gf). Evaluation is bounded by a fixed 47K future-paper pool, so truly novel contributions outside this window are invisible to our metric.

All experiments are limited to mathematics; generalisation to other domains with formal libraries (e.g. Coq, Isabelle) remains open. We also do not verify that generated theorems are formally provable — connecting generation to automated verification is a natural next step.
